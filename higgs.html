Gravitational waves from the Higgs field

Rice University | Supervised by Dr. Andrew Long

Project Overview

While gravitational waves (GW) are commonly associated with compact objects, we also expect that they are emitted from the Higgs field. We are motivated to detect GW because they serve as a window that allows us to infer the physics of the universe just seconds after the big bang. In this project, we calculate the gravitational wave energy density power spectrum resulting from the relaxation of the Higgs field in multiple eras of the universe. Our analysis will serve to determine whether or not these GW are detectable, and if they are, what we can learn about physical processes from 13 billion years ago.

Results

We calculated the GW energy density spectrum resulting from the relaxation of the Higgs field in multiple eras of the universe. Our spectrum was manipulable and could be modified to simulate theoretical and known dominant energy densities in the universe, as well as the time these eras ended and the vacuum expectation value (VEV) of the field we wanted to investigate. We found that gravitational waves resulting from the relaxation of the Higgs field are far from detectable. However, we have found that GW due to the GUT field are powerful enough to be registered by contemporary GW detectors. The figure to the left shows the GW energy density power spectrum for GW emitted from a self-ordering scalar field with a GUT scale VEV in a theoretical kination (kinetic energy) dominated era and the radiation dominated era.
