About Me

I'm Brandon Khek, a fourth-year astrophysics and math student at Rice University. I am an undergraduate interested astroparticle physics, early and late universe cosmology, string theory, and quantum gravity. Besides having a physics background, I am also well versed in upper-level undergraduate mathematics such as complex analysis and differential geometry. My extensive experience with computers and programming such as Python, Mathematica, and Unix makes me a versatile researcher as I prepare for a career in theoretical physics research.

Research

As an undergrauate, I have been very fortunate in having opportunities to engage in astrophysics research at SLAC, JPL, and Rice. I have explored my curiosities in areas such as machine learning, large-scale structure, and early universe cosmology. I would like to move toward research in high energy theoretical physics which includes areas like inflationary physics, quantum gravity, and the nature of dark energy. I also find joy in presenting my work to others communicating all of the cool things that I've learned! Below are some highlights of my research experiences and a few associated outcomes.

Machine Learning in Gamma Ray Astronomy

The low MeV gamma ray sky is poorly measured, motivating the proposal of a new instrument, GammaTPC. I worked on optimizing the instrument's ability to resolve gamma ray sources with convolutional neural networks through the reconstruction of Compton scattering events within the observatory.

  • Machine learning
  • Gamma ray astronomy
  • Python programming

Spherical Fourier Bessel (SFB) Analysis for Galaxy Surveys

Upcoming deep, wide-angle galaxy surveys such as SPHEREx and NGRST give us a lot of data to analyze. In this summer, fellowship, I maximize the extraction of cosmological information from these surveys by creating code to measure the theoretical SFB power spectrum of density fluctuations in the universe.

  • Large-scale structure
  • Galaxy surveys
  • Julia programming

Electromagnetic Signatures of Axions from Neutron Stars

Axions, a hypothetical candidate for what comprises cold dark matter, interact via electromagnetism very weakly. This gives the a possibility of the axions undergoing conversion into detectable photons. Here, I perform calculations of the probabilty of this occurring.

  • Dark matter
  • Field theory
  • Mathematica

Gravitational waves resulting from Higgs field inhomogeneities

Gravitational waves serve as a window into the very early universe. In this project, I explored the observational prospects of primordial gravitational radiation emitted from phase transitions in the early universe.

  • Early universe cosmology

Teaching

Coming into Rice, I was uncertain with what I wanted to study in college. The guidance of countless students, mentors, and professors enabled me to pursue opportunities that helped me define my interests. I hope to provide help to others as many have done to me.

COLL 106: Dissecting Physics Pop Science

COLL is a class code, short for COLLege course, which are student-taught courses. After a semester of training, Rice students develop and teach a COLL course on anything they'd like. In my course, I aim to give deeper insight on physics popular science topics, such as the Higgs boson and black holes by exploring the mathematical formulation behind them. The course is officially listed here.

RCEL ELITE Tech Camp

Over the summer of 2020 I had the pleasure of teaching 28 high school students machine learning in Python through RCEL - the Rice Center for Engineering Leadership. We used anomaly detection algorithms to analyze meteorological data. Examples include the Isolation Forest and Local Outlier Factor algorithms, aided with powerful libraries like SciKitLearn and MatPlotLib. Following up from this experience, I continued to teach with an organization called Rice Splash, where students at Rice can teach middle/high school students about any topic they want. I taught in the fall 2020 semester the basics of single variable calculus.
