About Me

I'm Brandon Khek, a third year physics PhD student at the University of Pennsylvania. Previously, I studied astrophysics and mathematics at Rice Univeristy. My research nowadays is primarily concerned with black hole perturbation theory and understanding the theoretical footing of inflationary models.
Besides having a physics background, I was trained in pure math including complex analysis and differential geometry. I also have extensive experience with programming in Python, Julia, Mathematica, and Unix.

Research

As a graduate student, I've been very fortunate to study areas of physics that are physically and mathematically rich. I recently finished a line of inquiry in understanding hidden structure of black hole spacetimes, and am now working on projects on multiple aspects of inflation, both in model building and symmetry breaking. I have had research experience at SLAC, JPL, and Rice in diverse areas such as machine learning, large-scale structure, and axion phenomenology. I find joy in presenting my work to others and take pride in being able to explain it clearly. Below are some highlights of my research experiences and a few associated outcomes.

Hidden structure of acoustic black holes

Spherically symmetric black holes at the classical level seem to be one of the simplest objects we could study. In this series of work, I study with collaborators that there exists a symmetry that cannot be easily visualized These symmetries govern the behavior of the black holes nonetheless and become manifest only at a mathematical level.

  • Black hole perturbation theory
  • Conformal symmetry
  • Mathematica

Machine Learning in Gamma Ray Astronomy

The low MeV gamma ray sky is poorly measured, motivating the proposal of a new instrument, GammaTPC. I worked on optimizing the instrument's ability to resolve gamma ray sources with convolutional neural networks through the reconstruction of Compton scattering events within the observatory.

  • Machine learning
  • Gamma ray astronomy
  • Python programming
Visit Project Page →

Spherical Fourier Bessel (SFB) Analysis for Galaxy Surveys

Upcoming deep, wide-angle galaxy surveys such as SPHEREx and Roman will give us a detailed 3D view of the distribution of matter. In this project, I maximize the extraction of cosmological information from these surveys by creating code to estimate the matter power spectrum.

  • Large-scale structure
  • Galaxy surveys
  • Julia programming

Teaching

Despite knowing that I enjoy physics, I have had a variety of uncertainties in my path to graduate school. The guidance of countless students, mentors, and professors enabled me to pursue opportunities that helped me define my interests. I hope to provide help to others as many have done to me.

Penn Physics and Astronomy Directed Reading Program (DRP)

I founded and ran the first iteration of a DRP in physics and astronomy at UPenn. I built it from the ground up, which involved tasks like securing funding for graduate student mentor stipends, soliciting undergraduate and graduate applications in physics classes and clubs, and coordinating DRP meetings for mentees and mentors to get to know each other. I am mentoring one student this semester.

COLL 106: Dissecting Physics Pop Science

COLL is a class code, short for COLLege course, which are student-taught courses. After a semester of training, Rice students develop and teach a COLL course on anything they'd like. In my course, I aim to give deeper insight on physics popular science topics, such as the Higgs boson and black holes by exploring the mathematical formulation behind them. The course is officially listed here.

RCEL ELITE Tech Camp

Over the summer of 2020 I had the pleasure of teaching 28 high school students machine learning in Python through RCEL - the Rice Center for Engineering Leadership. We used anomaly detection algorithms to analyze meteorological data. Examples include the Isolation Forest and Local Outlier Factor algorithms, aided with powerful libraries like SciKitLearn and MatPlotLib. Following up from this experience, I continued to teach with an organization called Rice Splash, where students at Rice can teach middle/high school students about any topic they want. I taught in the fall 2020 semester the basics of single variable calculus.
